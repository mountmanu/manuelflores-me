# Talk Like TED
**Author:** Carmine Gallo | **Category:** Public Speaking / Presentations

## Core Thesis
The world's most compelling speakers — as exemplified by TED talks — succeed by combining emotional passion, novel ideas, and memorable delivery into presentations that feel less like pitches and more like genuine human connections.

## Key Frameworks
- **The Three Secrets of TED:** Every great TED talk is (1) Emotional — the speaker is passionate and makes the audience feel something; (2) Novel — it teaches the audience something new or challenges existing assumptions; (3) Memorable — it uses stories, visuals, and demonstrations that stick well after the talk ends.
- **The Rule of Three:** Structure any presentation around exactly three core messages. The human brain finds three-part structures easy to process and recall; anything beyond three dilutes retention.
- **The 18-Minute Rule:** The optimal length for a focused, high-impact talk is 18 minutes — long enough for substance, short enough to maintain full cognitive engagement. Cognitive backlog builds after 18 minutes of sustained attention.
- **HSAM / Jaw-Dropping Moment:** Deliberately engineer one shocking statistic, unexpected demo, or counterintuitive revelation per talk — a "holy smokes" moment — to spike attention and give the audience a memorable anchor.
- **The 65% Rule (Visuals over Text):** Slides should be image-dominant, not bullet-heavy. Research cited by Gallo shows people learn 65% more from images paired with spoken words than from text on a slide read aloud.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Lead with passion, not expertise.** Audiences give sustained attention to speakers who visibly love their subject. Before building your deck, write a one-sentence statement of why you personally care about this topic — and open with it explicitly.
2. **Twitter-test your core message.** Before writing any content, compress your entire talk into a single tweet (140 characters). If you cannot, the message is not clear enough yet. This single sentence becomes your north star for every slide and story you include.
3. **Use personal stories relentlessly.** The brain reacts to narratives by releasing oxytocin, increasing trust and retention. Every major point should be anchored to a concrete story — ideally first-person or a specific customer/person story, never a hypothetical.
4. **Practice out loud for 200 hours (the deliberate practice standard).** TED speakers who appear effortlessly natural have typically rehearsed their 18-minute talk dozens of times aloud. Silent mental rehearsal does not wire the same neural paths as speaking. Record yourself and review.
5. **Engineer the jaw-dropping moment.** Identify the single most surprising data point or live demonstration in your material and build toward it as a deliberate reveal. Bill Gates releasing mosquitoes. Jill Bolte Taylor holding a human brain. The moment must be visual and visceral, not just verbal.
6. **Master the 18-minute constraint actively.** When given 30 or 60 minutes, still identify your core 18-minute spine. The remaining time is Q&A or bonus depth — the essential argument must survive at 18 minutes. This discipline forces you to cut weak content.
7. **Use multi-sensory delivery: vary pace, pitch, and pause.** Monotone delivery kills even strong content. Slow down and pause before and after key statements (the pause signals importance to the brain). Raise energy for stories, drop it for data. A pause of 2-3 seconds feels eternal to the speaker but normal to the audience.
8. **Replace text slides with single powerful images or props.** For every bullet-point slide you currently have, ask: "What image or object conveys this idea?" Gallo cites studies showing that bullet-heavy slides actively suppress recall. One striking image per concept outperforms six sub-bullets every time.

## SE/Presales Application
In enterprise SaaS demos and discovery calls, SEs are effectively TEDx speakers with a product — the 18-minute rule, Twitter-test message, and jaw-dropping moment translate directly: open every demo with a single sharp "why this matters to you" sentence, engineer one live product moment that surprises the buyer (unexpected speed, a workflow they assumed required manual work), and replace feature-list slides with a single ROI visual or customer story that anchors the entire narrative.
