# New Sales. Simplified.
**Author:** Mike Weinberg | **Category:** Sales / New Business Development

## Core Thesis
Most salespeople fail at new business development not because of talent or market conditions, but because they lack a simple, repeatable system for proactively hunting net-new accounts — and instead hide behind reactive busywork and existing accounts.

## Key Frameworks
- **The New Business Development Framework (Hunt-Farm Split):** Weinberg's central premise that "hunting" (net-new acquisition) and "farming" (growing existing accounts) require fundamentally different mindsets, skills, and time allocation — and that most sellers default to farming because it's safer and easier.
- **The Sales Story (Compelling Narrative):** A concise, customer-facing statement of who you help, what problems you solve, and what outcomes you deliver — not a product pitch, not a feature list. Used in calls, emails, and voicemails. The most underleveraged weapon in a seller's toolkit.
- **The Target List (Strategic Account Selection):** A deliberately short, written list of 25-50 high-fit prospects the seller commits to pursuing proactively. Without a written list, "prospecting" becomes reactive and random. The list forces prioritization and accountability.
- **The Proactive Outreach Cadence (Phone + Email):** Weinberg's prescribed rhythm of multi-touch, multi-channel outreach — starting with the phone call (not email), using voicemail strategically, and following up with short, direct emails. Volume and persistence matter as much as craft.
- **The Sales Call Execution Model:** A framework for running discovery and first meetings: arrive with an agenda, open with your Sales Story, ask pre-planned issues-based questions, avoid pitching too early, and close the meeting with a defined next step — never leave without one.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Write down your target accounts.** A mental list doesn't exist. Commit to 25-50 named accounts in writing, review them weekly, and ruthlessly remove accounts that aren't a genuine fit. The act of writing forces strategic thinking about who you actually want to win.
2. **Lead with the phone, not email.** Cold email has conditioned buyers to ignore outreach. A direct phone call — even to voicemail — is more memorable, more human, and more likely to generate a response than another message in an already-flooded inbox.
3. **Build a Sales Story before you prospect.** You cannot prospect effectively if you can't articulate in two sentences why a specific type of buyer should care about talking to you. The Sales Story is not about features — it names the business problems you solve and the outcomes clients achieve.
4. **Stop "checking in."** "Checking in," "circling back," and "touching base" emails are noise. Every outreach must offer a reason to engage — a relevant insight, a customer result, an industry trigger — or it signals that your time is worth nothing.
5. **Separate hunting time from everything else on your calendar.** Block dedicated, recurring prospecting time — ideally in the morning — and protect it from internal meetings, account management, and admin. Weinberg calls these blocks "sacred." Without protected time, hunting never happens.
6. **Run discovery before you demo or propose.** Rushing to solution mode before understanding the prospect's situation, pain, and political landscape is the single most common cause of lost deals and wasted proposals. Questions about issues, impact, and decision process must precede any presentation.
7. **Always leave a meeting with a defined next step.** "I'll follow up" is not a next step. A next step is a specific action, owned by the prospect or the seller, with a date attached. No defined next step means no deal momentum — it's a polite stall.
8. **Your attitude about prospecting is a choice.** Weinberg is blunt: salespeople who say they "hate cold calling" or "cold calling doesn't work" are rationalizing avoidance. Persistence, genuine curiosity about the prospect's business, and a belief that you can help — not charm or scripts — are what make outreach effective over time.

## Key Quotes
> "The phone is your most powerful prospecting weapon, and most salespeople are afraid to use it."

> "You cannot manage what you don't measure, and you cannot pursue what you haven't named. Write down your target accounts."

> "The fastest way to kill a deal is to start pitching before you've done any discovery. Buyers buy solutions to their problems, not features of your product."

## SE/Presales Application
As a Sales Engineer, you are often pulled into deals late and handed incomplete discovery — Weinberg's framework is a direct reminder to demand a defined Sales Story and a written target account strategy from your AE partners before investing demo or POC effort. More directly, SEs who own technical champion development should apply the proactive cadence and issues-based questioning model to their own outreach with technical buyers, not just wait to be introduced.
