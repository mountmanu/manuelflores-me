# High Output Management
**Author:** Andy Grove | **Category:** Management / Operations / Leadership

## Core Thesis
A manager's output is the output of their team and the teams they influence — therefore, the only way to increase your value is to systematically identify and remove the limiting steps that constrain your team's production, and to multiply your time through leverage.

## Key Frameworks
- **The Production/Breakfast Factory Model:** Every knowledge-work process is a production process with raw inputs, transformation steps, and a "limiting step" (the slowest or most constrained step). Identify the limiting step first, then build the rest of the process around it to avoid wasted effort upstream and downstream.
- **Managerial Leverage:** Output = Activity × Leverage. High-leverage activities affect many people or future outcomes (e.g., a 1-hour training session that benefits 10 engineers is worth 10 hours of individual coaching). Low-leverage activities — doing work a subordinate should do — are the manager's primary waste.
- **Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM):** The correct management style for any individual on any task is determined by their experience and confidence on *that specific task*, not their general seniority. Low TRM → directive (tell, prescribe). Medium TRM → coaching (explain, discuss). High TRM → delegate (set goals, stay out of the way). Mismatching style to TRM is the single most common management error.
- **The Meeting as Production Tool:** Meetings are not overhead — they are the primary mechanism for managerial work. Grove distinguishes *process-oriented* meetings (one-on-ones, staff meetings, operation reviews — recurring, structured) from *mission-oriented* meetings (ad hoc, convened to reach a specific decision). Both have a defined purpose and should be run with the same rigor as a manufacturing process.
- **The Decision-Making Framework (Free Discussion → Clear Decision → Full Support):** Every decision must pass through three phases. Discussion must be free and candid before the decision; once made, the decision must be clearly communicated; afterward, all team members must support it publicly even if they disagreed — "disagree and commit." Skipping any phase causes either bad decisions or passive sabotage.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Find the limiting step in every process you own.** Before adding resources or effort, map the process and identify what is actually constraining throughput. Adding capacity before the bottleneck is pure waste. This applies equally to a sales cycle, a product launch, or a hiring pipeline.
2. **The one-on-one is your highest-leverage recurring meeting.** Its purpose is information transfer *from* the subordinate *to* the manager, not the reverse. The subordinate should own the agenda. One hour per week with each direct report pays dividends in early problem detection that no amount of status reports can replace.
3. **Indicators must be paired.** Any single metric can be gamed or misleading. Pair output metrics with quality metrics (lines shipped vs. bug rate), and leading indicators with lagging ones. Running a business on a single KPI is like driving using only the speedometer.
4. **Delegate tasks at the *edge* of a person's capability, not inside their comfort zone.** People grow by being stretched on tasks where they have medium-to-high TRM. Delegating only easy tasks is managerial abdication; delegating tasks beyond someone's TRM without support is setting them up to fail.
5. **Your calendar is your production schedule — treat it that way.** Batch similar work, protect maker time, and aggressively eliminate low-leverage interruptions. Identify which recurring activities are "managerial leverage multipliers" and ruthlessly cut the rest.
6. **Training is the highest-ROI management activity.** A manager who trains 10 people and improves their output by 10% has effectively created a full additional person's worth of output. Grove argues managers should personally deliver training, not delegate it to HR, because context and credibility matter.
7. **Dual reporting and matrix structures are a feature, not a bug.** In complex organizations, individuals often report to both a functional manager and a business-unit manager. The tension is intentional — it forces tradeoffs to surface explicitly rather than be resolved unilaterally. The key is clear rules for who owns which decisions.
8. **"Peter Principle" failures happen because TRM assessments don't travel with people.** When someone is promoted, their TRM on the new role resets to zero regardless of their prior mastery. A newly promoted manager must be managed directive-style on managerial tasks even if they were a high-TRM individual contributor. Ignoring this destroys both the person and the team.

## Key Quotes
> "The output of a manager is the output of the organizational units under his or her supervision or influence."

> "A manager's own productivity depends on choosing to perform tasks with high leverage — those where an hour of work has the most impact on the output of the surrounding organization."

> "The single most important resource that we allocate from one day to the next is our own time."

## SE/Presales Application
In a presales role, your "output" is not the demo you deliver — it is the aggregate quality of every deal your influence touches, including enablement sessions you run for AEs, discovery frameworks you document, and proof-of-concept templates you build once and reuse across the team. Apply Grove's leverage lens ruthlessly: a one-hour technical workshop that sharpens five AEs' qualification skills outweighs five individual deal-rescue calls, and identifying the limiting step in your sales cycle (usually discovery depth, not demo quality) tells you exactly where to invest your preparation time.
