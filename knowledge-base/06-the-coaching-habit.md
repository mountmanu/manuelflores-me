# The Coaching Habit
**Author:** Michael Bungay Stanier | **Category:** Leadership / Management / Coaching

## Core Thesis
Most managers give advice when they should ask questions. Seven specific questions — used consistently — make you dramatically more effective as a coach and leader by helping people solve their own problems rather than creating dependency on you.

## Key Frameworks
- **The Seven Essential Questions:**
  1. The Kickstart: "What's on your mind?"
  2. The AWE Question: "And what else?"
  3. The Focus: "What's the real challenge here for you?"
  4. The Foundation: "What do you want?"
  5. The Lazy: "How can I help?" (not "let me help by...")
  6. The Strategic: "If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?"
  7. The Learning: "What was most useful for you?"
- **The Advice Trap:** The instinct to give advice immediately creates dependency, reduces people's capability, and often solves the wrong problem.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. "And what else?" is the highest-value coaching question — it surfaces the real issue beyond the first answer.
2. Giving advice feels helpful but often solves the presenting problem rather than the underlying one.
3. The best coaches create thinkers, not followers. Ask before advising.
4. "What do you want?" directly cuts through vague complaints to actionable issues.
5. The strategic question ("what are you saying no to?") is powerful for prioritization and focus.
6. Coaching conversations should be short, focused, and action-oriented — not therapy sessions.
7. The habit of asking one good question and staying quiet is harder than it sounds and more valuable than it looks.
8. The shift from "tell mode" to "ask mode" multiplies team capability over time.

## Key Quotes
> "Stay curious a little longer. Rush to action a little more slowly."
> "The advice trap is the instinct to give advice even when it's not wanted, needed, or useful."
> "Ask one question. Wait for the answer. Ask another question."

## SE/Presales Application
Applies to how SEs run discovery calls and mentor junior team members. The Kickstart question ("what's on your mind?") is the best opening for any discovery session. AWE ("and what else?") surfaces hidden technical requirements.
