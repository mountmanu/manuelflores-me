# Founding Sales
**Author:** Pete Kazanjy | **Category:** Startup Sales / Founder-Led Sales

## Core Thesis
Founders must personally own and master early sales before delegating it — not because they have no choice, but because doing so produces the customer insight, repeatable process, and hiring bar that makes scaling possible at all.

## Key Frameworks
- **The Artifact-Driven Sales Process:** Every stage of the sales motion should produce a concrete artifact (call script, objection doc, deck, email template) that can be handed off to a first hire without losing fidelity. Intuition doesn't transfer; written artifacts do.
- **The Hypothesis-Driven Pitch:** Treat your first pitch as a falsifiable hypothesis — you believe this persona has this pain solved by this capability. Each call either confirms or invalidates the hypothesis, updating the pitch for the next rep.
- **The Qualification Scorecard (BANT+ Variant):** Budget, Authority, Need, and Timeline are necessary but insufficient. Kazanjy adds organizational fit (can they actually use this?), urgency driver (what forces action now?), and competitive context. Document scores per prospect to build a pattern.
- **The Conversation-to-Close Funnel:** Map every stage — outreach, connect, discovery, demo, proposal, negotiation, close — with entry/exit criteria and expected conversion rates. Measuring each stage reveals where the process breaks, not just whether deals close.
- **The Sales Hiring Benchmark:** Before hiring rep #1, the founder must have closed at least 10 deals personally and documented the full motion. The hire's job is to replicate a known process, not discover one.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Founders must sell first, no exceptions.** Hiring a VP of Sales or a "sales guy" before the founder has personally closed 10+ deals nearly always fails — you don't yet know what you're asking them to replicate, and they can't build a process from scratch without the product feedback loop you have.
2. **Your pitch is a hypothesis, not a presentation.** Frame every early sales conversation as an experiment: state your assumed pain, assumed severity, and assumed solution fit. When prospects push back, you've learned something more valuable than a closed deal.
3. **Nail ICP before scaling outreach.** Ideal Customer Profile must be specific enough to reject prospects — company size, vertical, tech stack, team structure, growth stage. Spraying to a vague ICP wastes pipeline capacity and produces misleading conversion data.
4. **Discovery is the job, demo is the reward.** Spend the majority of first meetings asking questions to confirm or disprove your pain hypothesis. The demo should show only the capabilities that address the specific pain surfaced in that call — not a feature tour.
5. **Objections are a lagging indicator of discovery failure.** If you consistently hear "too expensive" or "not the right time," the root cause is almost always that you pitched before confirming real pain. Build an objection log, trace each objection back to its discovery gap, and fix the question that should have caught it.
6. **Artifacts are the unit of repeatability.** Write down your best discovery questions, your email sequences, your objection responses, and your qualification criteria while they're fresh. A verbal hand-off to your first hire degrades like a game of telephone; a written artifact scales.
7. **The demo must map to the org chart.** In enterprise SaaS, economic buyers, technical evaluators, and end users have different fears. Run separate demo flows for each persona, emphasizing ROI for the buyer, security/integration for the evaluator, and workflow improvement for the user.
8. **Founder credibility is a perishable asset — use it deliberately.** Early customers buy partly because the founder is involved. Use that to get honest feedback, deep access, and reference agreements. Document everything before you hand accounts to a CSM; that institutional knowledge is the foundation of your playbook.

## Key Quotes
> "Founders that try to skip the founder selling phase and go straight to hiring sales people are making a mistake that will cost them months, if not the company itself."

> "Your pitch is actually a hypothesis. And like all good hypotheses, it needs to be tested — and updated based on what you learn."

> "Every artifact you create while selling is a gift to your future sales team. Don't hoard the knowledge in your head."

## SE/Presales Application
SEs often inherit a demo and a pitch built by someone who no longer sells — Kazanjy's artifact-first approach gives SEs a framework to reverse-engineer a rigorous discovery process from the demos they already run: document the discovery questions that make a demo land, build persona-specific demo flows, and turn objection patterns into coaching material that strengthens the whole team's technical proof motion.
