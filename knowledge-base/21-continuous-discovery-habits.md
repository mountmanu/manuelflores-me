# Continuous Discovery Habits
**Author:** Teresa Torres | **Category:** Product Management / Customer Discovery

## Core Thesis
Product teams must replace periodic, project-based customer research with a weekly cadence of small customer touchpoints — automated, recurring, and tied directly to the opportunity space they are working in — so that discovery becomes a muscle rather than an event.

## Key Frameworks
- **Opportunity Solution Tree (OST):** A visual map that links a desired outcome (business goal) to the customer needs/pain points that serve it (opportunities), then to the potential solutions beneath each opportunity, and finally to the experiments that validate those solutions — preventing teams from jumping straight to solutions before understanding which opportunity is worth pursuing.
- **Continuous Interview Cadence:** The discipline of conducting at least one customer interview per week per product trio (PM, designer, engineer), using an automated recruiting pipeline so conversations are never blocked on scheduling logistics.
- **Assumption Mapping:** Before running experiments, teams surface all assumptions baked into a solution and plot them on a 2x2 of importance vs. unknownness to identify which assumptions to test first.
- **Story-Based Interviewing:** Instead of asking customers about features or preferences, interviewers elicit specific past experiences ("Tell me about the last time you…") to surface latent needs, workarounds, and context that customers cannot self-report in the abstract.
- **Product Trio:** The recurring cross-functional unit of PM + designer + one engineer that attends discovery together, ensuring discovery is a shared team habit and not a PM-only handoff artifact.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Automate your recruiting pipeline first.** Set up a persistent opt-in link (via email footers, in-product prompts, or Calendly) so interview candidates arrive continuously without manual sourcing — this is the single structural change that makes the weekly cadence sustainable.
2. **Always interview around a specific outcome, not a product area.** Anchor every interview to the one outcome your team is currently measured on (e.g., "reduce time to first value"), so every conversation generates relevant opportunities rather than a diffuse wish list.
3. **Map opportunities before evaluating solutions.** The OST forces teams to articulate the full customer opportunity space first; teams that skip this step pick solutions that address rare or low-impact pains and waste sprint capacity.
4. **An opportunity is not a solution in disguise.** "Customers want a dashboard" is a solution. "Customers don't know whether their team's activity is on track" is an opportunity. Reframe every stated preference into the underlying need to keep the solution space open.
5. **Test assumptions, not solutions.** Before building, write down every assumption your solution requires to succeed (desirability, viability, feasibility, usability), pick the riskiest one, and design the smallest possible experiment to stress-test it — a story, a prototype, a fake door, not a sprint.
6. **Interview for stories, not opinions.** Questions like "Would you use X?" produce unreliable answers; questions like "Walk me through the last time you tried to do X" produce concrete behavioral evidence. The past predicts behavior; the hypothetical does not.
7. **Synthesize in snapshots, not documents.** After each interview, capture two or three opportunity phrases on a sticky note and place them on the OST immediately — waiting to synthesize produces a backlog that never gets done and breaks the cadence.
8. **Measure discovery output, not discovery activity.** The leading indicator is the quality and specificity of opportunities on the OST, not the number of interviews conducted. A well-specified opportunity ("sales reps lose deals because they can't show ROI in the meeting") is actionable; a vague one ("customers want better reporting") is not.

## Key Quotes
> "Continuous discovery is not about doing more research. It is about making small, frequent contact with customers a habit so that the team is always learning, never catching up."

> "The Opportunity Solution Tree is not a planning tool — it is a thinking tool. Its job is to make your assumptions visible so you can challenge them before you build."

> "When you ask customers what they want, they will describe solutions. Your job is to hear the need underneath the solution they describe."

## SE/Presales Application
Sales Engineers spend more time with prospects at the point of technical evaluation than any other team member, making them the richest source of live opportunity data in the company — applying story-based interviewing techniques during discovery calls and logging verbatim pain statements (not feature requests) to the product team's OST directly shortens the feedback loop between market signals and roadmap decisions. In competitive deals, framing the demo around a single specific outcome the prospect is measured on (mapped from their own language in the discovery call) replicates the OST logic in real time: you anchor the solution to a validated opportunity rather than showcasing features speculatively.
