# The Presentation Secrets of Steve Jobs
**Author:** Carmine Gallo | **Category:** Presentations / Storytelling

## Core Thesis
Great presentations are not slide decks — they are theatrical experiences built around a single, Twitter-sized headline idea, delivered through story, simplicity, and rehearsed passion that makes the audience feel something before they think anything.

## Key Frameworks
- **The One-Sentence Headline:** Every presentation must be reducible to one clear, specific sentence (the "headline") that captures the entire value proposition — written before a single slide is made.
- **The Three-Act Structure:** Organize every presentation into exactly three parts (problems/villain → solution/hero → call to action), mirroring classical narrative so the audience can follow effortlessly.
- **The Villain-and-Hero Narrative:** Personify the problem as a villain (e.g., "clunky, bloated software") and your product as the hero that defeats it — this creates emotional stakes and makes the demo feel like a rescue.
- **The Rule of Three:** Cluster all content into groups of three (three benefits, three proof points, three features) because the human brain processes and retains triads best.
- **Zen Aesthetic (Simple Slides):** Slides are visual reinforcement, not speaker notes — one image or one large-text stat per slide, zero bullet-point paragraphs, whitespace as a design tool.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Write your headline first, slides last.** Before opening PowerPoint, write one sentence your audience will tweet. Everything in the deck either supports that sentence or gets cut. Example: "Today Apple reinvents the phone." That sentence is the entire presentation strategy.
2. **Open with the enemy.** Name the painful status quo in the first 60 seconds. Jobs called pre-iPod music players "random devices that hold 1,000 songs — big deal." Audiences lean in when they recognize their own frustration reflected back at them.
3. **Sell the benefit, not the feature.** "1,000 songs in your pocket" is a benefit; "5 GB hard drive" is a feature. Every feature claim must be translated into a human outcome before it goes on a slide or out of your mouth.
4. **Build to a dramatic reveal.** Structure your demo so the single most impressive moment lands after tension has built — Jobs pulled the MacBook Air from a manila envelope. Choreograph your wow moment; do not bury it in the middle.
5. **Use analog numbers to make data land.** Abstract numbers are forgettable; contextualized numbers are memorable. "We sold 4 million iPhones — that's 20,000 a day, every day since launch." Always anchor a stat to a human-scale reference point.
6. **Make it look easy through ruthless rehearsal.** Jobs rehearsed for weeks. The "effortless" keynote is a manufactured illusion. Rehearse transitions, demo flows, and recovery from failures (he planned for live-demo crashes) until the presentation feels spontaneous.
7. **Use the "but/therefore" connective tissue, never "and/then."** Each section should flow as "the world works like X, BUT that creates problem Y, THEREFORE our solution does Z." This keeps narrative tension alive; "and then... and then..." kills it.
8. **Create an intermission with a "just one more thing" structure.** Signal natural endings, let the audience relax, then deliver an unexpected bonus moment. This pattern resets attention and makes the closing beat disproportionately memorable.

## Key Quotes
> "Every presentation, whether it's a Macworld keynote or a pitch to a venture capitalist, must answer the one question that every member of the audience is asking: 'Why should I care?'"

> "Sell dreams, not products. Steve Jobs never sold computers. He sold tools to unleash human potential."

> "If you can't explain your idea in ten words, you don't know your subject well enough."

## SE/Presales Application
In an enterprise SaaS demo, the SE is the presenter — open every discovery call or demo with a one-sentence headline that names the prospect's villain (e.g., "Manual data reconciliation is costing your ops team 20 hours a week"), structure the demo in three acts (pain → product hero moment → proof), and rehearse the single most impressive product capability as a choreographed reveal rather than a feature walkthrough, so the buying committee feels the solution before the champion has to justify it internally.
