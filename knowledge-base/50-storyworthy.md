# Storyworthy
**Author:** Matthew Dicks | **Category:** Storytelling / Communication

## Core Thesis
Every compelling story is about a single moment of transformation — a "5-second moment" where a person changes in some meaningful way. All craft in storytelling exists solely to make that transformation land with maximum impact.

## Key Frameworks

- **The 5-Second Moment:** The atomic unit of every story — the precise instant of change, realization, or shift. Identify this moment first; the entire story is built backward from it to make it inevitable and forward to show its consequence.
- **Homework for Life:** A daily 2-minute practice of mining your day for one story-worthy moment and recording it in a spreadsheet. Over time this builds an inexhaustible personal story library and trains the brain to recognize narrative potential in ordinary experience.
- **Elephant, Payoff, Backpack:** Three structural tools — the Elephant signals what the story is about early so audiences know where they are going; the Payoff is the promised emotional resolution; the Backpack loads the audience with stakes and worry so they are invested in the outcome.
- **Cinderella Story Structure:** Begin as close to the end as possible, then use a "but/therefore" spine (not "and then") to chain scenes — each beat creates conflict or consequence that drives forward to the 5-second moment. Contrast between the character's beginning state and end state is what creates meaning.
- **Present Tense / Always in Scene:** Tell stories in the present tense, always placing yourself physically in a scene (location, sensory detail, weather, objects). Never summarize what happened — inhabit the moment so the audience experiences it alongside you.

## The 20% That Matters Most

1. **Find the 5-second moment before you write a word.** Ask: "What is the moment when something changed for me?" If you cannot name a specific instant, you do not yet have a story — you have an anecdote. Everything else (setup, context, tension) is scaffolding to make that instant hit hard.
2. **Start your story as close to the end as possible.** Audiences tolerate setup only because they trust a payoff is coming. Cut every scene that does not directly increase stakes or change the direction of the story. The opening sentence should be a scene, not preamble.
3. **Use "but" and "therefore" to chain beats, never "and then."** "And then" is the hallmark of summary. "But" signals conflict; "therefore" signals consequence. This forces each scene to earn its place by creating or resolving tension.
4. **Establish the Elephant early.** Within the first minute of a story (or first paragraph of text), signal what kind of story this is and what question it will answer. This is a promise that reduces anxiety and earns patience from the audience.
5. **Load the Backpack to build dread and investment.** Give the audience information the protagonist does not have, or make them acutely aware of what is at stake, so they carry worry into every subsequent scene. Suspense lives in the gap between what the audience knows and what can go wrong.
6. **Use the "Homework for Life" practice daily.** Record one story-worthy moment every day in two sentences. The goal is not to produce finished stories — it is to retrain your perception so you stop discarding the small, emotionally charged moments that actually make the best stories. After one year you will have 365 seeds.
7. **Never say how you felt — show the physical.** Replace "I was terrified" with the specific bodily or behavioral detail: "I could not stop checking my phone." Emotion named is emotion neutered; emotion embodied is emotion transferred.
8. **Stories must be about you, not about the audience or your message.** A story weaponized to teach a lesson turns into a lecture the moment the audience senses it. Paradoxically, the most personal and specific story — told honestly, without a moral agenda — lands the universal lesson automatically because audiences locate themselves in the teller's vulnerability.

## Key Quotes

> "The most important moment of your story is the five-second moment of change. Everything else exists to make that moment as powerful as possible."

> "Storytelling is not about entertainment. It is about connection. And connection requires vulnerability, specificity, and a character who changes."

> "Find the story in your day, every day. Not the big moments — those are easy. Find the small ones. That is where the real stories live."

## SE/Presales Application

In enterprise SaaS demos and discovery calls, an SE's most powerful tool is a 90-second customer story that follows the Cinderella structure: open in a scene (a prospect like them, before the solution), load the backpack with the specific pain and stakes, then deliver the 5-second moment where the customer's world measurably changed — letting the prospect feel the transformation before seeing the product. Replace generic case-study summaries ("Company X achieved 40% efficiency gains") with present-tense, embodied scenes that make the pain and the shift visceral, and the product becomes the inevitable hero without the SE ever having to say so.
