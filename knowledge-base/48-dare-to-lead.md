# Dare to Lead
**Author:** Brené Brown | **Category:** Leadership / Vulnerability / Culture

## Core Thesis
Brave leadership requires the courage to be vulnerable — to show up uncertain, take risks without guaranteed outcomes, and have hard conversations — because armored leadership (self-protection at the expense of connection) kills trust, innovation, and engagement on teams.

## Key Frameworks
- **The Armory vs. Daring Leadership:** Leaders either armor up (perfectionism, cynicism, numbing, knowing-it-all) to avoid vulnerability, or dare to lead by choosing courage over comfort. Armor protects the ego but destroys psychological safety.
- **BRAVING Inventory:** A trust-building model with seven behaviors — Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault (confidentiality), Integrity, Non-judgment, Generosity. Trust is built in small moments, not grand gestures.
- **Rumble Tools:** Structured practices for having honest, grounded conversations about hard things — includes "the story I'm making up," painting done, and the "square squad" (a small group of people whose opinions you actually respect).
- **Values Clarification:** Identifying your top two core values (not a list of 10) and using them as behavioral guides, especially when values conflict or when you're under pressure to compromise them.
- **Curiosity vs. Armored Responses:** Replacing judgment and self-protection with genuine curiosity — asking "what is this person's story?" or "what do I need to learn here?" — as a moment-to-moment leadership discipline.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Name the story you're making up.** When you feel defensive or hurt, say out loud: "The story I'm making up is..." — this surfaces assumptions before they drive bad decisions or damaged relationships. Use it in hard conversations to separate facts from interpretations.
2. **Trust is built in marble jars, not buckets.** Trust accumulates through many small, consistent acts (remembering details, following through, keeping confidence) — not through one big show of loyalty. Know which behaviors fill or empty the jar.
3. **"Clear is kind, unclear is unkind."** Vague feedback, avoiding difficult conversations, and softening hard messages to protect feelings actually cause more harm than direct, honest communication. Say the hard thing with care, not euphemism.
4. **Paint done before the work starts.** Before delegating or starting a project, articulate explicitly what "done" looks like — the standard, format, scope, and deadline. Misalignment on the picture of done is the root of most frustration in collaborative work.
5. **Name your values and operationalize them.** Pick two core values (e.g., courage, integrity) and then list three specific behaviors that honor each one, and three that violate each one. Values without behavioral definitions are decorative.
6. **Acknowledge the elephant; don't manage optics.** When there's tension, failure, or a hard truth in the room, naming it directly ("I want to address what happened last week") resets the environment for real work — pretending it doesn't exist costs more in lost trust and energy than the discomfort of naming it.
7. **Vulnerability is not oversharing or weakness.** It is the willingness to act despite uncertainty and emotional exposure. In practice: sharing a genuine concern in a meeting, asking for help when you don't know something, or giving honest feedback are all acts of vulnerability — not performance.
8. **The square squad over the cheap seats.** Whose opinions about your work and character actually count? Write those names on a small piece of paper and keep it close. When criticism or self-doubt hits, check — is this person on the list? If not, their judgment does not govern your response.

## Key Quotes
> "You can choose courage or you can choose comfort, but you cannot choose both."

> "Clear is kind. Unclear is unkind."

> "The story I'm making up is... [this is how you start the hard conversation]."

## SE/Presales Application
In enterprise SaaS presales, daring leadership shows up when an SE admits in a live demo that they don't know the answer rather than bluffing, or when they give a champion direct, honest feedback that the deal is at risk — using "clear is kind" to have the tough conversation a sales rep may avoid. The BRAVING trust model is directly applicable to building credibility with technical champions: reliability and accountability (doing exactly what you promised between calls) are the behaviors that separate a trusted advisor from a vendor rep.
