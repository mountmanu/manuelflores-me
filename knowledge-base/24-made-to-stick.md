# Made to Stick
**Author:** Chip & Dan Heath | **Category:** Communication / Messaging / Persuasion

## Core Thesis
Ideas fail not because they are bad, but because they are communicated badly; sticky ideas share six predictable traits (SUCCESs) that make them understandable, memorable, and motivating enough to change minds and behavior.

## Key Frameworks
- **SUCCESs (the six traits of sticky ideas):** Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Story — a checklist for diagnosing why a message fails and which lever to pull to fix it.
- **The Curse of Knowledge:** Once you know something, you cannot imagine not knowing it — the single biggest enemy of clear communication; experts systematically overestimate what their audience already understands.
- **The Gap Theory of Curiosity:** Curiosity is triggered not by novelty but by a perceived gap between what we know and what we want to know; create the gap before filling it.
- **The Sinatra Test:** If your idea can prove itself in one extreme, high-stakes example ("If I can make it there, I can make it anywhere"), it passes credibility without needing statistics.
- **Human-Scale Statistics:** Abstract numbers do not move people; anchoring a statistic to a single, relatable human story or physical comparison makes it viscerally real (e.g., "1 child dying" vs. "thousands dying").

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Lead with the core, not the context.** Find the single most important thing and say it first. Southwest's core message is "THE low-fare airline" — every decision filters through that anchor. Before any demo or pitch, write one sentence that must survive even if everything else is cut.
2. **Break a schema to create attention.** The brain ignores the expected. Open with a surprising but true fact, a counterintuitive claim, or a question the audience cannot immediately answer. Unexpected = attention on demand.
3. **Replace abstractions with concrete images.** "Velcro memory" sticks through sensory specificity. "Acidity levels of pH 3.5" is abstract; "as acidic as vinegar" is concrete. Always translate technical specs into things the audience can picture, touch, or taste.
4. **Use anti-authority for internal credibility.** When you lack brand authority, a single vivid, verifiable detail (the "Sinatra Test") or a credible anti-authority (a skeptic who became a believer) can outperform a wall of data.
5. **Tap "feel something" over "know something."** People act on feelings, not information. Identify the one emotion you want the audience to feel (fear of the status quo, pride in the solution) and engineer your story toward that feeling rather than toward comprehension.
6. **Tell a single-person story, not a demographic.** The "Mother Teresa Effect": statistics numb, but one named person with a specific problem creates empathy and action. In every executive presentation, replace aggregate pain metrics with one customer's story.
7. **Use the Velcro Theory of Memory: multiple hooks.** A message sticks when it connects to something the audience already knows. Use analogies, schemas, and categories they recognize — do not assume they will build the mental model themselves.
8. **Spot and destroy your Curse of Knowledge in real time.** Before finalizing any slide deck or email, ask: "What would someone who has never seen this product think that phrase means?" then rewrite every answer that exposes assumed knowledge.

## Key Quotes
> "The Curse of Knowledge: when we are given knowledge, it is impossible to imagine what it was like to lack that knowledge."

> "A sticky idea is understood, it's remembered, and it changes something — opinions, behaviors, values."

> "Statistics are rarely meaningful in and of themselves. Statistics will, and should, almost always be used to illustrate a relationship. It's more important for people to remember the relationship than the number."

## SE/Presales Application
Every discovery call, demo, and technical proof-of-concept is a stickiness problem: the SE has deep product knowledge (Curse of Knowledge) but the buyer has a five-minute attention window. Apply SUCCESs by opening demos with an Unexpected gap ("most teams assume X — here is what actually happens"), anchoring technical capabilities to a single concrete customer story (Emotional + Story), and replacing benchmark slides with one Sinatra-Test example that proves the hardest use case — leaving the room with one sentence the champion can repeat to the economic buyer without your help.
