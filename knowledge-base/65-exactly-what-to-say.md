# Exactly What to Say
**Author:** Phil M. Jones | **Category:** Sales / Influence / Language

## Core Thesis
The words you choose in a sales or influence conversation are the single biggest lever you control — a small set of "magic words" reliably bypasses resistance, opens minds, and moves people toward a decision without triggering defensiveness.

## Key Frameworks
- **Magic Words:** Specific phrases that work with the brain's automatic decision-making patterns rather than against them; they feel collaborative rather than pushy and reduce the listener's perceived risk.
- **The Assumptive Frame:** Structuring questions and statements so the outcome is already assumed, shifting the conversation from "whether" to "which" or "when" (e.g., "When would be a good time..." rather than "Would you like to...").
- **The Embedded Choice:** Offering two options both of which move the conversation forward, eliminating the binary yes/no trap (e.g., "Would it make more sense to do X or Y?").
- **Softening Preludes:** Opening phrases that lower resistance before a question or challenge ("I'm not sure if it's for you, but...", "You might not be ready for this yet...") that paradoxically increase engagement through reverse psychology.
- **The Momentum Close:** Using phrases like "Just one more thing..." or "Before I let you go..." to reopen or extend a conversation at a moment when the other person's guard is already down.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **"I'm not sure if it's for you, but..."** — This phrase removes pressure by signaling you're not desperate to sell. The listener relaxes and leans in. Use before any offer or idea you want someone to consider.
2. **"How open-minded are you?"** — Almost no one says "not very." This question commits the listener to being receptive before they know what you're about to propose, making rejection psychologically inconsistent with their self-image.
3. **"Just imagine..."** / **"Picture this..."** — Moves the listener from evaluating facts to experiencing a future state. Emotion-driven visualization precedes logical justification; get them feeling the outcome before defending it with logic.
4. **"What do you know about X?"** — Before pitching, this question surfaces existing beliefs, objections, and knowledge gaps. You stop talking past people and start from where they actually are.
5. **"Would it be helpful if...?"** — Reframes your offer as a service rather than a sales pitch. The listener says yes to being helped, not to buying something, lowering the psychological cost of agreement.
6. **"Most people in your position..."** — Social proof delivered this way is non-pushy; it normalizes the decision and shifts the burden of proof onto not acting. Specificity (industry, role, situation) dramatically increases its power.
7. **"Just one more thing..."** — A Columbo-style phrase used at the natural end of a conversation when defenses are lowest. Use it to re-introduce a key concern, a closing ask, or an upsell without restarting a formal pitch.
8. **Replace "if" with "when"** — "When you get started..." presupposes a positive decision has already been made. This single word swap in follow-up language subtly anchors the listener in an assumptive frame without feeling manipulative.

## Key Quotes
> "The most important words you will ever choose are the ones you use when talking to yourself — but the second most important are the ones you use with others."

> "It's not what you say, it's what they hear. Your job is to control what they hear by being deliberate about what you say."

> "People don't like to be sold, but they do love to buy — your language should make them feel like the decision is entirely theirs."

## SE/Presales Application
In discovery and demo conversations, an SE's words do as much work as the product itself — phrases like "Would it be helpful if I showed you how X handles that?" or "Most teams in your space handle this by..." reduce defensiveness and position the SE as a trusted advisor rather than a vendor. The assumptive frame and embedded-choice techniques are especially powerful during technical objection handling, where saying "Would it make more sense to address the security question now or after we walk through the integration?" keeps momentum without forcing a confrontation.
