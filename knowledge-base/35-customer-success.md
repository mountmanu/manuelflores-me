# Customer Success
**Author:** Nick Mehta, Dan Steinman & Lincoln Murphy | **Category:** Customer Success / SaaS Retention

## Core Thesis
In a subscription economy, the sale is just the beginning — revenue is won or lost at renewal, and the entire go-to-market motion must be redesigned around ensuring customers achieve their Desired Outcome, because a customer who doesn't succeed will churn regardless of contract length.

## Key Frameworks
- **Desired Outcome (DO):** Every customer has a Required Outcome (what they need to accomplish) plus an Appropriate Experience (how they need to accomplish it given their segment, sophistication, and budget). CS exists to deliver the intersection of both, not just feature adoption.
- **Customer Success Qualified Lead (CSQL):** Expansion revenue sourced by CS from customers who have achieved success and are therefore ready to buy more — the CS team's equivalent of an SQL, and a primary metric for tying CS to revenue growth.
- **Health Score:** A composite, leading-indicator metric (product usage, support ticket volume, NPS, engagement with CS team, contract coverage) that predicts churn risk before the renewal conversation; replacing lagging indicators like NPS alone.
- **Segmented CS Motion (High-Touch / Low-Touch / Tech-Touch):** Customer segments defined by ARR and complexity dictate the level of human engagement; high-ARR accounts get dedicated CSMs, mid-tier get pooled CSMs, and low-ARR get automated digital journeys — each with distinct playbooks and success criteria.
- **Churn Taxonomy (Predictable vs. Surprise Churn):** The book distinguishes churn that a healthy CS system should have caught (predictable — health signals deteriorated) from genuine surprise churn, forcing teams to diagnose whether their monitoring or their execution failed.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Define "success" before the contract is signed.** Success criteria must be captured in the sales cycle and handed off to CS at close; without a documented Desired Outcome, there is no objective basis to prove value at renewal or defend against a competitor.
2. **Time-to-Value is the highest-leverage metric in the first 90 days.** The fastest way to kill retention is a slow onboarding. Every day between contract signature and first realized value increases churn probability — measure it and set internal SLAs around it.
3. **Adoption is not success; outcome achievement is.** A customer who logs in daily but hasn't hit their business goal is still a churn risk. Track business outcomes (revenue influenced, hours saved, tickets deflected) alongside product usage, not instead of it.
4. **Build the Renewal 90 days before the renewal date, not 30.** If a CSM discovers a problem at 30 days out there is no time to fix it. The QBR cadence should surface risk at 90+ days so there is runway to course-correct before legal and procurement cycles begin.
5. **CS must own a revenue number — expansion or net retention — to earn organizational credibility.** CS teams that operate as pure cost centers get cut in downturns; those tied to Net Revenue Retention (NRR) or CSQL pipeline become strategic assets with budget and headcount protection.
6. **The CSM-to-customer ratio is a product and process architecture decision, not a headcount decision.** Scaling CS profitably requires investing in in-product onboarding, automated health alerts, and self-service resources so that human CSM time is reserved for moments that only humans can handle (executive relationships, complex escalations, expansion conversations).
7. **Executive Sponsorship programs are a CS responsibility, not a sales perk.** Mapping and nurturing relationships one level above the primary contact insulates against champion turnover — the single most common trigger of preventable churn in enterprise accounts.
8. **Churn is a lagging indicator of a product, sales, or CS failure that happened months earlier.** Post-mortems on churned accounts almost always reveal a missed health signal, an oversold promise, or a gap between what was sold and what was onboarded — use churn data to fix the upstream process, not just the renewal motion.

## Key Quotes
> "The subscription model has shifted power irrevocably to the customer. They can leave. And if you haven't made them successful, they will."

> "Customer Success is not a department. It is a company-wide commitment to ensuring that customers achieve their Desired Outcome through their interactions with your company."

> "Churn is almost always the result of something that went wrong long before the renewal conversation — the renewal is just when you find out about it."

## SE/Presales Application
SEs set the success trajectory before CS ever engages: the demo scenarios you choose, the use cases you validate, and the success metrics you co-create with the champion in the proof-of-concept all become the baseline against which the customer measures value at renewal. Documenting Desired Outcomes and handing them off in the post-sale brief is one of the highest-leverage things an SE can do to protect NRR and generate CSQLs downstream.
