# Drive
**Author:** Daniel Pink | **Category:** Motivation / Management / Performance

## Core Thesis
The carrot-and-stick model of motivation (Motivation 2.0) is obsolete for complex, creative work; sustained high performance comes from intrinsic motivation built on three elements: Autonomy, Mastery, and Purpose (Motivation 3.0).

## Key Frameworks
- **Motivation 2.0 vs. 3.0:** The old OS runs on extrinsic rewards and punishments; Motivation 3.0 is the upgrade that recognizes humans have a third drive — the inherent satisfaction of the work itself — which dominates when basic compensation is fair.
- **The AMP Model (Autonomy, Mastery, Purpose):** The three pillars of intrinsic motivation. Autonomy = control over Task, Time, Technique, and Team. Mastery = the urge to get better at something that matters. Purpose = connection to a cause larger than oneself.
- **Type I vs. Type X Behavior:** Type X (extrinsic) behavior is fueled by external rewards and tends toward short-term performance with long-term burnout. Type I (intrinsic) behavior is fueled by internal satisfaction, correlates with higher performance, greater well-being, and ethical conduct over time.
- **The Goldilocks Task / Flow:** Peak motivation occurs at the edge of one's current ability — tasks too easy cause boredom, too hard cause anxiety. Mastery-oriented work design keeps people in the flow channel.
- **Profit Motive + Purpose Motive:** Organizations that articulate a genuine "purpose maximizer" alongside profit maximizer attract Type I talent and outperform those that treat purpose as window dressing.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **"If-then" rewards destroy intrinsic motivation for non-routine tasks.** Contingent rewards ("do X, get Y") narrow focus, reduce creativity, and produce only short-term compliance — damaging exactly the behaviors complex sales engineering requires.
2. **Autonomy over the four T's drives engagement.** Give people control over Task (what they work on), Time (when), Technique (how), and Team (with whom) and discretionary effort rises dramatically without managerial overhead.
3. **Mastery follows deliberate practice, not talent.** The path is sustained effort just beyond current ability with immediate feedback — not aptitude. Pink cites Ericsson's 10,000-hour research to emphasize that mastery is always asymptotic; you approach it but never arrive.
4. **"Asymptotic mastery" is itself motivating.** The pursuit matters more than arrival. Framing a role as an endless, meaningful skill journey (not a destination) keeps Type I people engaged long after external rewards plateau.
5. **Baseline pay must be adequate first.** Pink is explicit: intrinsic motivation kicks in only after compensation is perceived as fair. Under-pay people and AMP collapses regardless of how much autonomy or purpose you layer on.
6. **Extrinsic rewards work for algorithmic (routine) tasks.** The nuance most readers miss: if-then rewards are appropriate for simple, rules-based work with a clear right answer. The failure is applying them to heuristic, creative, or relationship-driven work.
7. **"Now that" rewards (unexpected, non-contingent) are safe.** Unexpected bonuses or praise given after the fact do not damage intrinsic motivation the way pre-announced contingent rewards do. Recognize contribution without conditioning behavior on the reward.
8. **Purpose language in organizational messaging shifts behavior.** Teams exposed to purpose-framed goals (patient outcomes, customer transformation) measurably outperform teams given only profit-framed goals, even when the underlying work is identical.

## Key Quotes
> "The secret to high performance and satisfaction — at work, at school, and at home — is the deeply human need to direct our own lives, to learn and create new things, and to do better by ourselves and our world."

> "Rewards can deliver a short-term boost — just as a jolt of caffeine can keep you cranking for a few more hours. But the effect wears off — and, worse, can reduce a person's longer-term motivation to continue the project."

> "Mastery is a pain. It demands effort, grit, and deliberate practice. But the path to mastery — becoming ever better at something you care about — is not a treadmill. It is fulfilling."

## SE/Presales Application
SEs who frame every demo and discovery call around the customer's *purpose* (the transformation they are trying to create, not the product features) activate the same intrinsic-motivation levers in champions that Pink describes — making champions self-motivated internal sellers rather than passive evaluators. Internally, SE leaders who grant autonomy over discovery technique, demo narrative, and proof-of-concept scope retain top technical talent far more effectively than quota-pressure or SPIFFs alone.
