# Thinking, Fast and Slow
**Author:** Daniel Kahneman | **Category:** Behavioral Economics / Decision Making

## Core Thesis
Human judgment is governed by two distinct cognitive systems — a fast, automatic, associative System 1 and a slow, effortful, deliberate System 2 — and most errors in judgment arise because System 1 operates invisibly and System 2 is lazy, accepting System 1's outputs without scrutiny.

## Key Frameworks
- **System 1 / System 2:** System 1 is automatic, emotional, and fast (pattern recognition, intuition, heuristics). System 2 is deliberate, logical, and slow (calculation, analysis, self-control). Most daily decisions are made by System 1, often without System 2 being aware.
- **Heuristics and Biases:** The mind uses mental shortcuts (availability, representativeness, anchoring) to make fast judgments. These shortcuts are adaptive but produce predictable, systematic errors in specific contexts.
- **Prospect Theory:** People evaluate outcomes as gains or losses relative to a reference point, not as absolute states of wealth. Losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains — loss aversion is a core feature of choice, not a bug.
- **WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is):** System 1 constructs the most coherent story possible from available information and ignores what it does not know. Confidence is a product of narrative coherence, not completeness of evidence.
- **Two Selves (Experiencing vs. Remembering):** The experiencing self lives moment-to-moment; the remembering self evaluates episodes by their peak intensity and their ending (Peak-End Rule). People make decisions based on the remembering self, often to the detriment of their actual experienced well-being.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Anchoring dominates numerical judgment.** The first number introduced in a negotiation or evaluation becomes a gravitational anchor. Even arbitrary, irrelevant numbers shift estimates significantly. Set your own anchor early and explicitly — do not let the other side anchor first.
2. **Loss aversion (~2x) is stronger than gain motivation.** Framing the same outcome as avoiding a loss rather than achieving a gain dramatically increases uptake. "You are currently losing $X per quarter" lands harder than "You could save $X per quarter."
3. **WYSIATI means incomplete information feels complete.** Decision-makers form confident conclusions from whatever subset of data they have. Controlling which information is presented first shapes the entire mental model they build — they will not notice what you omitted.
4. **Cognitive ease breeds liking and agreement.** When information is easy to process (clear fonts, familiar language, repeated exposure), System 1 tags it as true and good. When it is hard to process, skepticism increases. Simplicity in demos and proposals is not dumbing down — it is neurologically strategic.
5. **Availability bias inflates recent or vivid examples.** What is easily recalled feels more likely and more important. Concrete customer stories and recent failure anecdotes shape perceived risk far more than base-rate statistics — leverage this deliberately with reference stories.
6. **The Peak-End Rule governs memory of experiences.** How an experience is remembered depends almost entirely on its most intense moment and its final moments — not on duration or average quality. A demo that ends strongly is remembered as better than one that is uniformly good but fades out.
7. **Overconfidence is the most pervasive bias.** Experts consistently overestimate the accuracy of their predictions. Planning fallacy (underestimating time/cost/risk) is a direct consequence. Conduct pre-mortems — assume the project failed and ask why — to surface risks before commitment.
8. **Substitution is silent.** When System 1 cannot answer a hard question, it substitutes an easier question and answers that instead, without flagging the swap. Buyers who cannot evaluate technical superiority substitute "do I like this rep?" Likability and trust become proxies for product quality.

## Key Quotes
> "A reliable way to make people believe in falsehoods is frequent repetition, because familiarity is not easily distinguished from truth."

> "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is, while you are thinking about it."

> "The confidence that individuals have in their beliefs depends mostly on the quality of the story they can tell about what they see, even if they see little."

## SE/Presales Application
In enterprise SaaS deals, buyers use System 1 to form gut verdicts on fit and trust within the first minutes of a discovery call or demo — rational ROI analysis comes later as post-hoc justification. An SE who anchors the business case early (loss framing), engineers a strong demo ending, and simplifies complex architecture into a coherent narrative wins the System 1 verdict before procurement even opens a spreadsheet.
