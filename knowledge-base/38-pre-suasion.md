# Pre-Suasion
**Author:** Robert Cialdini | **Category:** Influence / Persuasion / Psychology

## Core Thesis
The most powerful persuasion happens before the message is delivered: by controlling what a person is thinking about in the moment just prior to your ask, you can dramatically increase their receptivity to it without changing the message itself.

## Key Frameworks
- **Pre-Suasion (Privileged Moment):** The brief window immediately before a message is delivered is the highest-leverage point for influence — whatever concept is made salient at that moment becomes the lens through which the subsequent message is evaluated.
- **Channeled Attention = Altered Perception:** Whatever we direct attention to is automatically granted heightened importance. Attention is not a neutral spotlight; it is a meaning-maker. Focus on pain → pain feels more significant; focus on opportunity → opportunity feels more significant.
- **Unity (7th Principle of Influence):** Beyond the original six principles (reciprocity, scarcity, authority, consistency, liking, social proof), Cialdini adds Unity — shared identity ("we-ness") with a person causes them to weight your requests more favorably than mere liking does.
- **Associative Activation:** Any concept, image, or environment that is introduced primes a network of related concepts, feelings, and behavioral tendencies. Exposure to an Italian map before wine selection shifted buyers toward Italian wines without their awareness.
- **Attention Managers (CAUSE framework):** The features most likely to capture and hold attention are: Contrast, Attractive visuals (faces/sex), Unfinished stories/tasks (Zeigarnik), Self-relevance, and Easy fluency violations (things that are slightly hard to process signal importance).

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Prime the criterion before making the claim.** Ask "Are you adventurous?" before pitching a new idea, or "Do you value results?" before presenting ROI — the person self-labels, then behaves consistently with that label. The question does the pre-suasion; the pitch confirms it.
2. **Open with a question that directs attention to the single strongest dimension of your offer.** If your product's best attribute is reliability, open the conversation on reliability as a category before showing your product. The audience will rank that attribute higher when they evaluate all vendors.
3. **Use openers that highlight what's at stake (loss) before you present the solution.** People are twice as motivated by potential losses as gains. A brief "what does it cost you to stay as-is?" primes loss-aversion and makes the solution feel more urgent — even if the solution pitch is identical.
4. **Place people in a "yes" state with small, effortless agreements before the main request.** Each "yes" activates consistency pressure. Starting a demo with lightweight confirmations ("You want shorter implementation cycles, right?") builds a runway of assent.
5. **Environment shapes response without awareness — control the meeting context.** Hard chairs produce harder negotiating stances; cluttered rooms produce scattered thinking; a clean, premium physical (or virtual) environment primes quality and seriousness. Optimize your demo environment, backgrounds, and slide aesthetics accordingly.
6. **Unfinished stories and open loops hold attention better than complete ones (Zeigarnik Effect).** Tease a key finding, a surprising result, or a case study outcome early in a presentation and delay the resolution — attention stays locked until closure is delivered.
7. **Unity beats liking as an influence lever.** Shared identity (same industry, same struggle, same city, same alma mater) creates a sense of "we," which generates compliance and trust at a deeper level than just being likable. Surface genuine shared identity early in a relationship.
8. **Reciprocity works best when it is meaningful, unexpected, and given first — without strings.** A genuine insight, a warm introduction, or a useful resource delivered before any ask creates obligation that outlasts the interaction and returns interest in the form of referrals, access, and goodwill.

## Key Quotes
> "The best persuaders become the best through pre-suasion — the process of arranging for recipients to be receptive to a message before they encounter it."

> "We don't recognize how profoundly the initial channel of our attention determines the road we then travel."

> "It's not enough to have a good message; you must give it at the right moment — and that moment can be engineered."

## SE/Presales Application
Before every demo or discovery call, a Sales Engineer should engineer the mental frame the prospect is in: open with a single question or statement that primes the evaluative lens you want them to use (reliability, speed-to-value, integration depth) so that when your product is shown, it is judged against the standard where it wins. Controlling the pre-meeting agenda, the first slide, and the opening question is not ceremony — it is the highest-leverage persuasion move available, because it happens before the prospect's critical faculties are fully engaged.
