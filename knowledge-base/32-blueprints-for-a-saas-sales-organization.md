# Blueprints for a SaaS Sales Organization
**Author:** Jacco van der Kooij & Fernando Pizarro | **Category:** SaaS Sales / Revenue Operations

## Core Thesis
SaaS companies fail not because of bad products but because they bolt traditional sales models onto a recurring-revenue business — the fix is designing a dedicated, metrics-driven sales machine purpose-built around the SaaS unit economics of Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC), Lifetime Value (LTV), and churn.

## Key Frameworks
- **The SaaS Sales Blueprint:** A stage-by-stage factory model where each revenue function (Marketing, SDR/BDR, AE, CSM) is a discrete unit with defined inputs, outputs, conversion rates, and capacity levers — optimized independently then connected into a single revenue pipeline.
- **Recurring Revenue Tiers:** Segments deals by ACV into Tier 1 (low-touch / product-led), Tier 2 (inside sales), and Tier 3 (enterprise/field) — each tier demands a different sales motion, headcount ratio, and sales cycle design.
- **The Bowtie Funnel:** Extends the classic funnel past the close into a "second half" covering onboarding, adoption, expansion, and renewal — revenue is generated on both sides of the knot, not just at the close.
- **Unit Economics Scorecard:** Uses CAC Payback Period, LTV:CAC ratio, Magic Number, and Net Revenue Retention (NRR) as the four dials that determine whether a sales organization is worth scaling; any scaling before a positive Magic Number (>0.75) destroys value.
- **Sales Capacity Planning Model:** Headcount decisions are driven backward from revenue targets through quota attainment rates, ramp time, and pipeline coverage ratios — not org-chart intuition.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Nail one tier before scaling.** Attempting to run all three tiers simultaneously before proving unit economics in at least one guarantees burn without signal — pick the tier that matches your current ACV and validate CAC Payback < 18 months first.
2. **Separate hunting from farming.** Combining new-logo acquisition (AE) and expansion/renewal (CSM) in a single role destroys both motions; the skills, incentives, and time horizons are incompatible — split them as soon as monthly recurring revenue justifies it.
3. **The SDR role is a math problem, not a hustle problem.** SDR productivity is entirely predictable from activity volume × conversion rates at each micro-stage (connect → discovery → qualified → meeting held) — if pipeline is short, diagnose which conversion rate broke, then fix the playbook for that stage.
4. **Ramp time destroys capacity plans.** A new AE hired in Q1 typically produces zero net-new revenue for 3–6 months; failing to account for ramp in headcount models leads companies to consistently miss targets even when they hit hiring goals.
5. **Churn is a sales problem disguised as a CS problem.** Churn caused by poor customer fit was usually created at the top of funnel — the ICP definition, the discovery call, or the AE's willingness to close unqualified deals; fixing NRR requires intervening in the sales motion, not just the renewal call.
6. **Quota design must match deal distribution.** Setting a single quota number when deal sizes follow a power-law distribution (a few big deals, many small) distorts forecasting; segment quotas by deal size band or apply weighted pipeline stages to get accurate coverage ratios.
7. **Onboarding is a revenue event.** Time-to-first-value during onboarding is the strongest predictor of renewal and expansion; CSMs and SEs who accelerate the first live use case directly increase LTV — this is measurable and should be a KPI.
8. **Build playbooks before you scale headcount.** Adding reps to an unscripted sales motion scales chaos; a documented discovery framework, objection-handling guide, and demo script must exist and be validated by at least two top performers before headcount doubles.

## Key Quotes
> "A SaaS sales organization is not a department — it is a revenue factory. Every stage must have defined inputs, outputs, and conversion rates, or you are not running a factory, you are running a hope."

> "The bowtie reminds us that the sale is not the finish line. The finish line is the renewal, and the only way to reach it is to engineer success from day one of onboarding."

> "Scaling a sales team before you understand your unit economics is not growth — it is an accelerated path to running out of cash."

## SE/Presales Application
In a Tier 2/3 SaaS motion, the SE is the highest-leverage conversion asset between technical discovery and closed-won — shortening demo-to-close cycles and raising technical win rates directly improves the Magic Number and reduces CAC. SEs who instrument their own conversion metrics (demo attendance → technical win → close) and tie demo outcomes to onboarding time-to-value become visible contributors to the unit economics the book says determine whether to scale.
