# Obviously Awesome
**Author:** April Dunford | **Category:** Product Positioning / Marketing

## Core Thesis
Positioning is not a tagline — it is the deliberate act of defining the context in which your product is understood, so the right buyers immediately see why it is the obvious best choice for them. Bad positioning is the root cause of weak sales, confused messaging, and demos that land flat.

## Key Frameworks
- **The 5 Components of Positioning:** (1) Competitive alternatives — what buyers would do if your product didn't exist; (2) Unique attributes — capabilities or features that only you have; (3) Value — what those attributes enable for the buyer; (4) Best-fit customers — who cares most about that value; (5) Market category — the frame that sets buyer expectations before you say a word.
- **The Positioning Spectrum:** Products exist on a spectrum from "new feature of existing product" to "new market category." Most companies create a new subcategory or reframe an existing one — claiming a new category is expensive and rare; repositioning within an existing one is usually the highest-ROI move.
- **The ABCs of Market Category:** Each market category carries a set of assumptions (what the product does, who uses it, what it costs, who it competes with). Choosing the right category is choosing which assumptions work in your favor — wrong category = uphill battle on every deal.
- **The 10-Step Positioning Process:** A team-based workshop sequence: list competitors → isolate unique attributes → map attributes to value → identify best-fit customers → choose market frame → test and lock. Done in a focused session with sales, marketing, and product together, not by marketing alone.
- **Transitional Positioning:** When launching in a new category, anchor the product to a known category first ("it's like X, but for Y") to reduce cognitive load, then reveal the differentiated layer. Buyers can't value what they can't categorize.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. Positioning is set by default if you don't set it deliberately — buyers will slot your product into whatever category feels closest, and you will be compared to competitors you never intended.
2. Start with competitive alternatives, not features. The question is not "what do we do?" but "what would the buyer do if we didn't exist?" — spreadsheets, a competitor, doing nothing. That answer defines your real competitive set.
3. Unique attributes only matter if they map to value the best-fit customer cares about. A feature that impresses engineers but does not change a business outcome is not a positioning asset.
4. Best-fit customers are not "everyone in the market" — they are the narrow segment for whom your unique value is a must-have, not a nice-to-have. Defining this segment makes sales faster and win rates higher.
5. Market category choice is the single highest-leverage positioning decision. The category sets price expectations, competitor comparisons, and the buying criteria before you say anything — pick wrong and you fight those assumptions on every call.
6. Positioning must be cross-functional. If sales, marketing, and product have different answers to "what are we and who is it for?", positioning has not been set — it has just been declared by one team and ignored by the others.
7. Features do not equal value. The positioning statement must express the outcome the buyer gets (faster close rates, reduced compliance risk, lower infrastructure cost), not the mechanism that delivers it.
8. Repositioning an existing product is harder than positioning a new one because internal stakeholders are anchored to the old story. The 10-step workshop process is designed specifically to surface and break that internal anchor.

## Key Quotes
> "Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares a lot about."

> "If you don't deliberately position your product, customers will do it for you — and they'll do a terrible job."

> "The market category you choose is like a costume your product wears. It changes everything about how people see it before you open your mouth."

## SE/Presales Application
SEs operate downstream of positioning — a well-positioned product means discovery reveals a tight fit quickly, demos map directly to stated buying criteria, and objections shrink because the competitive frame is already correct. When deals feel like swimming upstream (wrong competitors keep coming up, buyers don't understand the value, demos require heavy qualification every time), it is usually a positioning problem, not a demo problem — and an SE who can diagnose that earns credibility with product and marketing, not just the field.
