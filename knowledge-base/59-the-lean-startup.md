# The Lean Startup
**Author:** Eric Ries | **Category:** Startups / Product / Innovation

## Core Thesis
Most startups fail not from building the wrong thing badly, but from building the wrong thing well — the antidote is a scientific, iterative loop of validated learning that replaces opinion-driven roadmaps with evidence from real customers as fast as possible.

## Key Frameworks
- **Build-Measure-Learn Loop:** The fundamental engine of the Lean Startup — convert ideas into products (build), measure how customers respond (measure), and decide whether to persevere or pivot (learn). Speed through this loop is the primary competitive advantage.
- **Minimum Viable Product (MVP):** The smallest version of a product that enables a full cycle of the Build-Measure-Learn loop with minimum effort — its purpose is learning, not shipping features.
- **Validated Learning:** A unit of progress measured by running experiments that test specific business hypotheses against real customer behavior, not by output metrics like features shipped or lines of code.
- **Innovation Accounting:** A framework for measuring startup progress using actionable metrics — establish a baseline with an MVP, tune the engine toward the ideal, and decide to pivot or persevere based on whether metrics improve.
- **Pivot or Persevere:** A structured decision point — a pivot is a structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis, not a random change; persevering means continuing to iterate on the current approach.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Vanity metrics kill honest decisions.** Gross pageviews, total signups, and cumulative downloads always go up — they feel good but reveal nothing about whether the business is working. Replace them with cohort-based retention, activation rates, and revenue-per-customer to force honest conversations.
2. **The MVP is a learning instrument, not a product launch.** Its value is in disconfirming assumptions cheaply — an MVP can be a landing page, a concierge service, or a Wizard-of-Oz manual process. Shipping a polished product before validating demand is waste.
3. **Two types of hypotheses must be tested first: the value hypothesis (does this product deliver value?) and the growth hypothesis (will customers tell others?).** Everything else is secondary until both are confirmed.
4. **Five Whys drives root-cause investment decisions.** When a problem surfaces, ask "why?" five times to trace a symptom back to its systemic cause — then invest proportionally in fixing the root, not the symptom. This prevents over-investing in firefighting.
5. **Engines of growth are distinct and must be chosen deliberately.** The sticky engine (retain customers), the viral engine (customers recruit customers), and the paid engine (revenue funds acquisition) each have specific leading metrics — optimizing the wrong engine is invisible waste.
6. **Pivot types give language for strategic shifts.** Ries names specific pivot archetypes: zoom-in (one feature becomes the whole product), zoom-out (product becomes a feature), customer segment, customer need, platform, business architecture, value capture, engine of growth, channel, and technology. Naming the pivot type forces clarity about what hypothesis is actually changing.
7. **Large-batch vs. small-batch production is a trap.** The intuition that larger batches are more efficient is wrong — small batches surface defects immediately, reduce cycle time, and accelerate learning. This applies to software releases, marketing experiments, and sales playbooks.
8. **"Genchi gembutsu" (go and see for yourself) is mandatory before building.** Customer discovery must involve direct observation of customer behavior in their environment — secondhand reports and surveys produce the wrong product. Early customer interviews should be structured to surface problems, not validate pre-built solutions.

## Key Quotes

> "We must learn what customers really want, not what they say they want or what we think they should want."

## SE/Presales Application
In enterprise SaaS presales, the Lean Startup mindset reframes every discovery call and proof-of-concept as a Build-Measure-Learn experiment — the SE should treat each demo and POC as an MVP designed to test a specific hypothesis (e.g., "this workflow will eliminate the prospect's manual reconciliation step") and instrument the POC with success criteria upfront so the outcome is validated learning, not just a dog-and-pony show. Applying Innovation Accounting to the POC pipeline — tracking conversion rates by deal stage, POC-to-close ratio, and time-to-value — replaces vanity metrics (number of POCs run) with actionable signals that reveal whether the presales motion is actually working.
