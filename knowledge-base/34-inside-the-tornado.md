# Inside the Tornado
**Author:** Geoffrey Moore | **Category:** Technology Marketing / Market Adoption

## Core Thesis
After crossing the chasm, a technology market can enter hypergrowth — the Tornado — where a single vendor becomes the de facto standard and all buyers rush to adopt before being left behind; winning the Tornado requires abandoning niche customization, standardizing ruthlessly, and out-shipping every competitor.

## Key Frameworks
- **Technology Adoption Life Cycle (TALC):** Innovators → Early Adopters → Early Majority (Bowling Alley) → Late Majority (Tornado) → Laggards. Each segment has a completely different buying psychology and requires a different go-to-market posture.
- **The Bowling Alley:** Pre-Tornado growth strategy — win one vertical niche at a time, use each win as a reference to knock over the adjacent pin. Whole Product must be complete for each niche.
- **The Tornado:** Hypergrowth phase triggered when the Early Majority floods in all at once. The market selects a gorilla (dominant platform), a chimp (strong #2), and monkeys (niche survivors). Being the gorilla is the only defensible long-term position.
- **Gorilla / Chimp / Monkey Dynamics:** The gorilla captures disproportionate margin and controls the architecture. Chimps survive by riding the gorilla's platform and competing on price or features. Monkeys win only in verticals the gorilla ignores.
- **Main Street:** Post-Tornado phase when hypergrowth ends and the market commoditizes. Compete on customer intimacy, extensions, and ecosystem lock-in rather than speed of deployment.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **In the Tornado, distribution beats product.** The winner is the vendor that can get into the most accounts fastest — channel, partnerships, and sales capacity matter more than feature parity. An inferior product distributed wider wins.
2. **Tornado buying motive is fear of being left out, not ROI.** Late-majority buyers want to buy the market leader because the risk of picking the wrong vendor is greater than the risk of overpaying. Lead with market-share proof, analyst rankings, and customer count — not feature comparisons.
3. **Whole Product wins the Bowling Alley; speed wins the Tornado.** Pre-Tornado, you must deliver a complete solution for the niche (integrations, services, training). In the Tornado, ship fast and demand that partners complete the Whole Product — your job is volume.
4. **Standardize, not customize, during hypergrowth.** Every customer exception slows you down and lets competitors catch up. The Tornado is the one moment when the market tolerates a "take it or leave it" posture from the vendor.
5. **Gorilla pricing: charge full price, never discount on core.** The gorilla's margins come from pricing power. Discounting signals weakness and erodes the perception of market leadership — the exact asset you are selling in the Tornado.
6. **The chasm and the Tornado are two separate chasms.** Most companies mistake early Bowling Alley success for Tornado entry and over-invest in customization when they should be shifting to standardized volume. Recognize which phase you are in before setting strategy.
7. **Post-Tornado, the gorilla's enemy is itself.** Once on Main Street, the winning move is customer intimacy and upselling the installed base — the same standardize-and-ship discipline that won the Tornado now kills customer retention.
8. **Tornado market share is a self-fulfilling prophecy.** Buyers choose the perceived leader to reduce career risk ("nobody gets fired for buying IBM"). Every analyst mention, press release, and customer logo is part of a perception war, not just a PR exercise.

## Key Quotes
> "In the Tornado, the goal is not to satisfy the customer — it is to install your product before the competition installs theirs."

> "Gorillas don't win on merit. They win because the market decides it needs a gorilla and then makes one."

> "The Bowling Alley demands patience and customization; the Tornado demands ruthlessness and standardization. Most companies can't make the switch."

## SE/Presales Application
In the Tornado, SEs must shift from solution consultants to deployment accelerators — the deal is won on speed and market credibility, not feature depth, so demos should lead with adoption proof (logos, volume, analyst position) and compress time-to-technical-win rather than exploring edge cases. On Main Street, the SE role flips back: competitive differentiation comes from deep customer intimacy, vertical expertise, and expansion selling into the installed base.
