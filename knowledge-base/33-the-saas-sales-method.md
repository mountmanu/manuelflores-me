# The SaaS Sales Method
**Author:** Winning by Design | **Category:** SaaS Sales / Methodology

## Core Thesis
SaaS selling is fundamentally different from traditional software sales because revenue is earned repeatedly — the customer must succeed and expand, or the deal is eventually lost. Every sales motion must be engineered around customer outcomes, not features or one-time transactions.

## Key Frameworks
- **SPICED:** The discovery framework — Situation, Pain, Impact, Critical Event, Decision. Replaces BANT with outcome-focused qualification that maps to SaaS renewal economics.
- **The Impact/Cause Diagram:** Map the buyer's stated pain up to business impact and down to root causes. Deals close when the seller proves they address the root cause of a high-impact problem.
- **The Bowtie Funnel:** Extends the traditional funnel past the close into adoption, retention, and expansion. Revenue generation happens on both sides of the bowtie — new ARR and net revenue retention.
- **The SaaS Sales Process Stages:** Educate → Align → Validate → Propose → Commit → Onboard → Impact → Expand. Each stage has a specific exit criterion; skipping stages causes churn and failed expansions.
- **The Blueprint:** A structured deal-planning document that captures the buyer's situation, pain, impact, critical event, and success criteria — used internally to align AE, SE, and CSM on the same story before every customer interaction.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. Identify the Critical Event first: the prospect's hard deadline or forcing function (board meeting, contract renewal, go-live date) determines urgency and is the lever that moves deals. Without a Critical Event, pipeline stalls.
2. Pain without Impact is not a closed deal — always quantify what the pain costs the business (revenue lost, hours wasted, churn caused). Impact is what justifies budget allocation.
3. The SE's demo must connect every capability shown to a specific pain and its business impact already surfaced in discovery — never show a feature the prospect did not ask for or confirm they care about.
4. Deals are lost post-contract as often as pre-contract: if the customer doesn't achieve impact in the first 90 days, they don't renew or expand. Time-to-value is a sales metric, not just a CS metric.
5. The Decision framework (who decides, what process, what criteria) must be mapped explicitly — SaaS buying committees are larger than traditional software deals; missing a stakeholder loses the deal in committee.
6. Use the Impact/Cause Diagram to handle objections: when a prospect pushes back on price or timing, return to the impact figure you jointly agreed on and ask if it has changed.
7. Coach the champion to carry your message internally by giving them the SPICED story in writing — a one-pager that restates their pain, impact, and how you solve it in their own business language.
8. Expansion is planned at the close, not after onboarding: the initial contract should leave obvious value left on the table (additional seats, modules, use cases) so the expansion motion has a natural trigger.

## SE/Presales Application
The SE owns the Validate stage — turning a prospect's stated pain into a technical proof of impact via demo, POC, or pilot. SPICED gives the SE a consistent pre-call briefing format to ensure every technical interaction is anchored to Impact and Critical Event, not feature coverage. The Bowtie model also means the SE's work does not end at commit: a clean technical handoff to CSM with documented success criteria directly determines expansion ARR.
