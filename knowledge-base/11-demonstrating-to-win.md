# Demonstrating to Win
**Author:** Robert Riefstahl | **Category:** Presales / Demo Skills

## Core Thesis
Most demos fail not because the product is weak, but because the presenter shows features instead of solving the prospect's specific business problems — winning demos are built around pain, not functionality.

## Key Frameworks
- **The Differentiating Demo:** A structured approach where every demo segment is anchored to a specific business pain the prospect has acknowledged, making the product's value self-evident rather than explained.
- **The Demo Flow (Tell-Show-Tell):** Frame the capability before showing it ("Here's the problem you told us you have"), demonstrate it live, then confirm resolution ("That eliminates the manual reconciliation work your team does every Friday"). Never show without framing first.
- **Dominant Buying Motive (DBM):** Every buyer has one overriding reason to buy — uncover it during discovery and make it the spine of the entire demo. All other features are subordinate.
- **Potholes:** Riefstahl's term for the dangerous traps presenters fall into during demos — unexpected questions, competitor comparisons, feature gaps, and technical failures — and the scripted techniques to navigate each without derailing the flow.
- **The Proof Point:** A concrete, numbers-backed customer outcome inserted at the moment a capability is shown ("A company like yours cut invoice processing time by 60% with this workflow") — makes capability tangible without a case-study digression.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. **Discovery is 80% of the demo.** If you don't know the prospect's top 3 business pains before the demo starts, you have no basis for a winning presentation. Never demo without a pre-call.
2. **Lead with the pain, not the product.** Open by recapping the prospect's own words about their problems. This signals you listened, builds trust, and anchors everything that follows to their world.
3. **Never show a feature that doesn't map to a stated pain.** Every capability shown that isn't tied to a confirmed problem weakens the demo by diluting focus and inviting off-topic questions.
4. **Control the agenda out loud.** State the agenda at the start, confirm it with the audience, and name each transition ("Now let's move to the area you said costs your team the most time"). This prevents hijacking and keeps the demo on your story.
5. **Kill the pothole proactively.** When you know a gap or competitor question is likely, address it on your terms before it surfaces — a brief, confident acknowledgment followed by a redirect to your strengths is far stronger than a defensive response under pressure.
6. **Rehearse out loud, not in your head.** Riefstahl is explicit: silent run-throughs don't build muscle memory. Full verbal rehearsal with live software — including simulated potholes — is the only preparation that holds under prospect pressure.
7. **Use the "parking lot" with precision.** Off-topic questions derail flow; a named parking lot ("Great question — let me put that on our list and we'll address it at the end") respects the question without surrendering control of the narrative.
8. **Close the demo with a call to action, not a summary.** End by connecting what was shown to a concrete next step tied to the prospect's timeline and DBM — "Based on what you've seen, does this solve the invoicing problem well enough to move forward with a pilot?"

## Key Quotes
> "The number one mistake demonstrators make is showing the product rather than solving the customer's problem."

> "You are not presenting software. You are presenting a solution to a business problem. The software is just the proof."

> "A pothole left unhandled is a credibility crater. Know where they are and drive around them — or fill them before the prospect hits them."

## SE/Presales Application
For an enterprise SaaS SE, this book is a repeatable operating system: use discovery to build a pain-anchored script, structure every demo segment around the Tell-Show-Tell loop tied to the prospect's DBM, and rehearse pothole responses until they are reflexive — so that complex, multi-stakeholder demos stay on message even when a champion's boss asks a curveball question about a competitor or a missing feature.
