# Great Demo!
**Author:** Peter Cohan | **Category:** Presales / Demo Skills

## Core Thesis
Most demos fail because they show too much, too early, in the wrong order — starting with setup and context when buyers only care about seeing their specific problem solved. The antidote is the "Illustrations First" method: show the deliverable immediately, then explain only what's needed to understand it.

## Key Frameworks
- **Do the Last Thing First (Illustrations First):** Open the demo with the finished output — the report, the dashboard, the result — that directly addresses the prospect's top pain. Never bury the payoff at the end of a feature tour.
- **The Situation Slide:** Before any software, capture the prospect's world in one slide: their current situation, the pain, and the impact. This anchors the demo to their reality and kills irrelevant tangents.
- **The Three Levels of Demo:** Technical Demo (show everything) → Vision Demo (show the end result only) → Great Demo (show the specific result for this specific buyer, backed by just enough context). Great Demos live at level three.
- **SITDD (Summarize, Introduce, Transition, Demonstrate, Describe):** A five-step micro-structure for each demo segment — state the problem, name the capability, bridge to the screen, show it, narrate the business value.
- **The Moment of Value:** The single screen or workflow where the buyer feels "that solves my problem." Every Great Demo is engineered to reach this moment as fast as possible and linger there.

## The 20% That Matters Most
1. Open with the output, not the input. Show the finished dashboard or report in the first 90 seconds; everything else is backstory.
2. Limit each demo to one or two "critical business issues" (CBIs) — the pains with real cost or urgency. Showing more than two dilutes credibility and attention.
3. Ask during discovery: "What would you need to see today to feel confident moving forward?" This defines the Moment of Value before the demo starts.
4. Never show a feature without a preceding "so here's the problem that causes" setup. Features without problems feel like a product tour, not a solution.
5. Use the prospect's own data or a believable facsimile. Generic sample data breaks the illusion; familiar data creates instant recognition.
6. "Peel back the layers" only when asked — after reaching the Moment of Value, resist the urge to keep clicking. Wait for the buyer to pull you deeper.
7. Practice the "wow" path cold: the specific sequence of clicks from blank screen to Moment of Value should be rehearsed until it is automatic and free of dead ends.
8. End every demo with a "Vision Summary" — restate the two CBIs you addressed, confirm you solved them, and ask what the logical next step is. This converts demos into pipeline.

## Key Quotes
> "The best demos are short, focused, and leave the customer wanting more — not exhausted from seeing everything."
> "Do the Last Thing First: show them the finished result before you show them how to get there."
> "Most demos are product tours. A Great Demo is a business case delivered visually."

## SE/Presales Application
For an enterprise SaaS SE, Great Demo! is the operational playbook for every customer-facing software show: use the Situation Slide to lock the room onto one or two CBIs before opening the product, then drive straight to the Moment of Value using the prospect's own terminology and data — keeping the total runtime under 20 minutes for a first demo. The SITDD micro-structure applies equally to discovery demos, executive briefings, and POC readouts.
